A BIT ABOUT ME

San Francisco, CA

PURPOSE: Data Science

Make the world better through data

What drives my interest in data science is the ability for seemingly disparate numbers to weave a compelling story—a story that requires both quantitative analysis and qualitative reflection in order to be told.

I was born in July, 1999 in San Francisco. I have always been fascinated with understanding the world around me and early on I developed an aptitude for mathetmatics and natural sciences. In recent years I've realized that the way we understand the world is just as much about how the world works as it is about how we experience it.

I have taken an interest in data science to achieve a greater understanding of our world. I have taken an interest in communication in order to spread that understanding. And I have taken an interest in philosphy, how it pertains to the way the world affects us, and how in turn it illuminates the effects our actions have on the world.

We live at an incredible point in time, there is so much discovery right at our fingertips, but with those discoveries there are also many questions to be asked. I hope to be able to interpret those questions and look to the world for answers.

– Parker Addison

SOME RECENT UPDATES

UC San Diego: 12–15 October, 2018

SDHacks

I teamed up with two fellow undergrads to create a 3D graphic visualizer/editor for neural nets. We created a web application that turned a Keras model json into an interactive model that could be modified and exported as a json again.

We are currently planning a massive overhaul so that we can turn this project into a fully-functioning and useful public tool to help encourage deeper understand of neural nets and reduce the copy-and-paste epidemic.

UC San Diego: 06–07 October, 2018

UC Health Hack

The weekend before SD Hacks, I participated in the UC Health Hack 2018. I worked alongside a fellow classmate, as well as a pediatrician from Harvard, a database engineer, and a software architect. Working with this team was the first time I've seen the true power of team diversity first-hand.

We created an Amazon Alexa skill named "Ouch, That Hurts!" which allows patients to easily report symptoms without needing to keep a journal or call the doctor. By making it extremely easy to report such symptoms, we could collect a plethora of data that could be used to help predict and diagnose symptoms in the future.

I got my hands dirty with AWS Lambda and DynamoDB in order to establish persistence with a scalable database.

UC San Diego: September 2018

Discussion Leader

For my second quarter tutoring DSC 10, I have been tasked with creating and leading discussion sections.

This is an intense but extremely rewarding role—while the main purpose of a discussion section is to make sure that nobody falls behind, I make sure that everyone leaves my section understanding the material covered in lecture and better equipped to handle data science problems outside of the classroom.

I do this by presenting real datasets and asking real questions.

The thing I'm most happy about is the level of engagement expressed by the students who attend discussions—it's truly heartwarming to see them interested in applying what we're learning.

UC San Diego: April 2018

Instructional Assistant

After two quarters at UC San Diego I was offered an IA (instructional assistant) position for the third-ever offering of a new data science class, DSC 10: Principles of Data Science. I am excited to be part of a brand new major, and to be among the people who can open new students' eyes to the world of data science.

This quarter, the CSE Department didn't want the Data Science program taking all of the graduate student TA's, so I had to assume many of the roles of a TA such as: creating, revising, and grading homework and project assignments; grading exams, creating exam solutions, and proctoring exams; and participating heavily in course organization and management. I did this all in addition to holding office hours to tutor students.

Chapman University: April 2018

ASA DataFest

With a team of four other students in my major, we represented UC San Diego at the ASA (American Statistical Association) DataFest held at Chapman University. We had a midterm right before the event started—and the drive took a few hours—so after getting a late start we were in an exceptionally crunchy time crunch to fulfill what we were tasked with upon arrival.

We were given an anonymized dataset from indeed.com, and tasked to come up with an interesting question and create a short business pitch to present to indeed by the end of the hackathon. After plenty of stress and sleep depravation, we posed a question to find out what cities have the best prospects for different types of jobs and we created an algorithm to rank and recommend cities that are well suited for a given job industry.
